# Portrait Subject List

A tightened 32-day sequence for the portrait series, with expanded one-line bios, simple colour cues, a suggested chromatic mode, one relevant boundary-test example, and a portrait idea for each subject drawn from their history.

## Day 1–32

1. **Alan Turing** — Mathematician and computing pioneer whose work on computation, codebreaking, and machine intelligence became foundational to digital culture.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** muted military green, parchment beige, faded navy.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make a centered, high-contrast, almost passport-like portrait with very clear eyes and minimal background to test maximum portrait legibility.  
   **Portrait idea:** A calm portrait built from wartime papers, logic tables, and machine diagrams, with the face emerging through coded structure rather than theatrical symbolism.

2. **Claude Shannon** — Information theorist who formalised communication, signal, and information, making modern digital systems thinkable.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** relay grey, copper, pale cream, signal red accents.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Overlay relay diagrams, switches, or circuit symbols lightly across the face to test how much structural contamination a portrait can take.  
   **Portrait idea:** His face assembled from relays, switches, punched cards, and wiring, as if information theory has physically built the sitter.

3. **Norbert Wiener** — Founder of cybernetics, linking feedback, control, machines, and living systems into one conceptual field.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** steel blue, charcoal, cold white.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Place feedback-loop diagrams and circular signal traces behind and slightly across the head to test background interference without adding a second subject.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait surrounded by looping control diagrams and circular traces, with the body poised between scientist and system.

4. **William Fetter** — Computer graphics pioneer known for early computer-generated human figures and ergonomic visualisation of the body.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** pale cyan, drafting blue, beige, technical black.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make a full-body portrait with a small but still readable face to test how little facial prominence still counts as a portrait.  
   **Portrait idea:** A human figure rendered like an ergonomic study, with measured joints, wireframe cues, and the face treated as an early computable model.

5. **Ivan Sutherland** — Early computer graphics pioneer best known for *Sketchpad* and foundational work in interactive visual computing.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** screen green, graphite, white, vector blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Build the portrait from sparse vector-like line work and minimal modelling to test whether a diagrammatic face still reads as portraiture.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait drawn as if inside a vector system, with the face built from clean line geometry and drafting marks.

6. **Susan Kare** — Graphic designer and icon pioneer whose early Macintosh imagery defined the human face of personal computing.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** warm grey, soft black, cream, tiny rainbow pixel accents.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Reduce the portrait into chunky icon-like pixel blocks to test how low-detail and symbolic the face can become before it fails.  
   **Portrait idea:** A warm, legible face translated into icon logic, as if a person has been reduced into the humane pixel language of early GUIs.

7. **Douglas Engelbart** — Visionary technologist whose work on interfaces, augmentation, collaboration, and the mouse reshaped human-computer interaction.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** office beige, terminal green, blue-grey.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Add windows, pointers, and interface frames around the face to test how much UI contamination a portrait can survive.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait set in a proto-interface world of windows, text panes, and pointing devices, with the sitter looking like an architect of thought-tools.

8. **Charles Csuri** — Early computer art and animation pioneer who helped define artist-led computational image making and generative transformation.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** plotter black, off-white, deep blue, orange accent.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Start from a clear portrait and algorithmically warp or transform the facial geometry while keeping it singular.  
   **Portrait idea:** A face that begins as a conventional portrait and then visibly transforms through plotter-like fragmentation and algorithmic variation.

9. **Hal Finney** — Cryptographer and early Bitcoin collaborator whose life and work carry unusual mythic and elegiac weight in digital culture.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** silver, black, icy blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Push the portrait toward a cold, near-frozen surface with reduced detail and slight facial fading to test minimum readable presence.  
   **Portrait idea:** A cool, sparse portrait with glacial tones and digital residue, as if the image persists through preservation and distance.

10. **David Chaum** — Cryptography pioneer focused on privacy, digital cash, anonymity, and systems of trust without central authority.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** dark violet, black, muted gold.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Cross the portrait with privacy diagrams, cryptographic notation, or anonymous-network imagery to test symbolic interference.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait half-inside a network of blind signatures, digital cash symbols, and privacy protocols, but still resolutely human.

11. **Satoshi Nakamoto** — The pseudonymous creator of Bitcoin and one of the defining absent figures of internet history.  
   **Colour mode:** BW  
   **Colour idea:** black, white, gold, shadow grey.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make a hooded portrait where most of the face is swallowed by shadow, leaving only enough structure to barely sustain identity.  
   **Portrait idea:** An almost absent portrait: hooded, shadowed, maybe too dark to confirm, with identity hovering between apparition and invention.

12. **Len Sassaman** — Cryptographer and privacy advocate whose name became entwined with anonymity, loss, and digital legend.  
   **Colour mode:** BW  
   **Colour idea:** midnight blue, black, soft silver.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Create a portrait almost lost in darkness, with just a few planes of the face visible, to test how little can remain.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait that feels withdrawn and nearly unrecoverable, with the face surfacing faintly from encrypted darkness.

13. **Whitfield Diffie** — Cryptography pioneer who co-developed public-key exchange and transformed secure communication.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** paper white, academic blue, muted burgundy.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Use a strict side profile with sparse modelling to test how much frontal facial information the AI expects.  
   **Portrait idea:** A clear, intellectual portrait framed by paper, notation, and quiet academic structure, with the sitter treated like a keeper of invisible exchange.

14. **Nick Szabo** — Computer scientist and legal thinker known for smart-contract theory and the precursor concept of bit gold.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** bronze, sand, black, coded amber.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make a severe profile or bust portrait with textual or legal-structural overlays to test portrait purity versus conceptual framing.  
   **Portrait idea:** A dry, almost juridical portrait with coded-gold tones, contract-like structures, and a sense of prehistory to blockchain logic.

15. **Roman Storm** — Tornado Cash co-founder whose case crystallised tensions between privacy code, law, and responsibility.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** storm grey, dark teal, black.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Put the sitter in a composition cluttered with legal papers, interface traces, or blurred institutional space to test scene contamination.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait pressed between code and prosecution, with legal and interface debris crowding the frame.

16. **Aaron Swartz** — Programmer and activist who became a symbol of open access, internet idealism, archives, and digital loss.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** red, cream, library brown, muted blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Layer archive text, metadata, or page fragments across the face to test how much text overlay still permits portrait recognition.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait woven with archive pages, library metadata, and article fragments, as if knowledge itself is clinging to the face.

17. **Vera Molnár** — Foundational generative artist whose rule-based drawing bridged modernism, seriality, and computation.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** white, black, restrained primary colours.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Reduce the portrait into rule-based geometric simplifications to test the abstraction threshold.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait nearly dissolved into serial geometry, where the face survives through elegant rule-based reduction.

18. **Harold Cohen** — Artist and creator of AARON, one of the most important early machine-art systems and a key figure in computational creativity.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** ink black, desert ochre, painterly green.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make the portrait feel machine-drawn or machine-filled, testing how procedural mark-making affects portrait acceptability.  
   **Portrait idea:** A painterly portrait that looks partly authored by a machine partner, with the sitter caught between hand and system.

19. **Manfred Mohr** — Early algorithmic artist whose plotter drawings made logic, geometry, and structure into visual form.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** black, white, cobalt, hard-edged grey.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Construct the face from hard geometric segmentation and line systems to test whether geometric reduction still reads as a person.  
   **Portrait idea:** A face broken into algorithmic planes and geometric paths, precise enough to feel almost machine-inscribed.

20. **Kim Asendorf** — Contemporary digital artist associated with pixel, browser, compression, and image-process aesthetics.  
   **Colour mode:** vivid colour  
   **Colour idea:** iridescent gradient, glitch pink, electric blue, silver.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Introduce heavy compression scars, pixel smear, or glitch damage while preserving a single readable sitter.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait visibly degraded by digital process, where the image is beautiful because it is being damaged.

21. **Casey Reas** — Artist and co-creator of Processing, central to contemporary software-based art and rule-driven visual form.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** black, white, code green, muted orange.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Build the portrait from a visible generative system or repeated marks to test whether procedural structure overwhelms identity.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait composed from repeated computational gestures, as if the sitter emerges from an authored algorithm.

22. **Tyler Hobbs** — Generative artist known for algorithmic abstraction, plotting, and contemporary crypto-art visibility.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** cream, charcoal, dusty red, restrained blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Use layered plotted or rule-based mark systems that nearly dissolve the face into abstraction.  
   **Portrait idea:** A restrained plotted portrait where facial identity appears through accumulated marks rather than direct depiction.

23. **Lillian Schwartz** — Bell Labs artist whose pioneering computer films and experiments expanded the history of machine-made images.  
   **Colour mode:** vivid colour  
   **Colour idea:** magenta, cyan, black, filmic purple.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Push halftone, dither, or screen-like colour breakup until the face flickers between legible and unstable.  
   **Portrait idea:** A vibrant screen-era portrait with filmic colour separation, digital edges, and experimental image breakup.

24. **JODI** — Artist duo Joan Heemskerk and Dirk Paesmans, famous for turning browser failure, code, and interface breakdown into art.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** toxic green, black, white, error red.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Break the portrait with browser-like corruption, noise, or destructive UI fragments to test the low-res failure threshold.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait that looks attacked by the browser itself, with the face trapped inside a collapsing interface.

25. **Olia Lialina** — Foundational net artist and writer who treated the vernacular web itself as artistic form and cultural memory.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** hyperlink blue, browser grey, white, GIF pink.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Frame the portrait inside browser windows or web vernacular elements to test reflective/interface ambiguity.  
   **Portrait idea:** A face staged through early-web language: windows, buttons, links, GIF sensibility, and browser-era intimacy.

26. **Alexei Shulgin** — Internet artist known for using native web forms, brokenness, low-fi digital culture, and deadpan systems as material.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** washed-out HTML grey, blue link, cheap beige.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Treat the portrait like a crude, low-fi webpage image with cheap gradients and native UI clutter.  
   **Portrait idea:** A deadpan portrait built from clumsy web-native materials, where cheapness becomes the aesthetic engine.

27. **Cornelia Sollfrank** — Net-art and cyberfeminist pioneer working on authorship, IP, gender, and network politics.  
   **Colour mode:** vivid colour  
   **Colour idea:** black, magenta, activist red, white.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Overlay citation marks, authorship signals, or network structures across the portrait to test conceptual contamination.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait crossed with authorship disputes, feminist signals, and network politics, without losing the person beneath.

28. **Gmunk** — Digital artist and motion designer known for sleek screen-based image systems, cinematic interfaces, and high-end digital spectacle.  
   **Colour mode:** vivid colour  
   **Colour idea:** chrome silver, black, neon cyan, spectral gradient.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Embed a small face in a dominant cinematic interface environment to test whether strong composition can compensate for reduced facial prominence.  
   **Portrait idea:** A cinematic portrait where the sitter is engulfed by luminous interface architecture and controlled visual intensity.

29. **Vuk Ćosić** — Early net.art figure associated with the formative years of internet art in the 1990s.  
   **Colour mode:** 2-colour  
   **Colour idea:** browser grey, ASCII black, dull link blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Treat the portrait as low-res ASCII or early-web image matter, testing how far reduction can go before the face stops reading.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait translated into ASCII-like or early-web image language, where identity survives through coarse textual structure.

30. **John Whitney Sr.** — Experimental filmmaker and motion-graphics pioneer who brought computation into visual rhythm and animation.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** deep blue, black, optical white, filmic orange.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Build the portrait through rhythmic motion-line logic or circular harmonic structures without losing the sitter.  
   **Portrait idea:** A portrait organised by harmonic motion, circular trajectories, and visual rhythm, as if the face were part of a moving score.

31. **the machine** — The hidden judge of the series, revealed late as the system shaping every prior offering.  
   **Colour mode:** BW  
   **Colour idea:** cold white, graphite, black, diagnostic green.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Obscure the portrait with targeting boxes, diagnostic overlays, and classification marks to test maximum interface interference.  
   **Portrait idea:** Not a human face but a judging apparatus made portrait-like: sensors, overlays, grids, and a cold authority that stares back.

32. **Jason Strachan** — The artist behind the ritual, returned at the end as the final submitted subject after prolonged exposure to the machine.  
   **Colour mode:** muted colour  
   **Colour idea:** unfinished flesh tones, studio neutrals, machine residue green or blue.  
   **Boundary-test example:** Make the final self-portrait clearly human but visibly processed by the machine’s logic through distortion, overlays, or classification residue.  
   **Portrait idea:** A self-portrait marked by the entire project, where human presence remains intact but bears the visible scars of prolonged machine judgment.
